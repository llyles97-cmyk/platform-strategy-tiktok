01 — The Attention System Framework

A Five-Layer Model
for Music Virality

Most virality frameworks stop at engagement metrics. This one goes further — separating the mechanics of attention capture from the mechanics of audio spread. On TikTok, a song can go viral without the artist ever posting a single video. That distinction is the whole point.

01
Hook
Entry Point

First 1–3 seconds. Stops the scroll. For music, this is which part of the song anchors the moment — drop, lyric, sample, or silence.

02
Retention
Watch Behavior

% watched, loops, rewatches. Does the format delay the payoff? Does the song create tension that holds the viewer through the end?

03
Engagement
Signal Strength

Saves → Shares → Comments → Likes. Is the comment section reacting to the song or to the video? That distinction changes the diagnosis.

04
Distribution
Algorithm Push

When TikTok decides to scale content. Organic sound spread vs. creator-driven. Platform-correlated artists see 11% week-over-week streaming growth vs. 3% for others.

05
Audio
Portability
Sound Travel
Differentiator

How far the sound travels without the original video. # of uses, stitch/duet behavior, meme adoption. This layer is what makes the framework music-native.

02 — Case Studies

Three Songs.
Three Mechanisms.

Each case study was selected to isolate a different layer of the framework. Together they cover the full spectrum of how music actually moves on TikTok.

01
Sabrina Carpenter
"Espresso" — 2024
Lyric-as-Meme Machine

The Setup

"Espresso" dropped April 11, 2024 — the same week Carpenter played Coachella. It peaked at #3 on the Billboard Hot 100, topped charts in 20+ countries, and spent 65 weeks charting. Island Records (UMG) engineered the moment deliberately: Coachella slot, Taylor Swift tour support, Saltburn/Barry Keoghan cultural adjacency.

#3 Billboard Hot 100 Peak
65 wks Time on Chart
20+ Countries Charted #1
257M Spotify Streams (early)

Framework Breakdown

Hook

The opening melody is clean and Italo-disco-adjacent — but the real hook was the lyric. "That's that me espresso" is grammatically broken in a way that demands attention. It sounds like an error, which makes people stop.

Retention

The format that spread wasn't the music video. It was the transition video — Carpenter posted a TikTok of herself doing a rapid wardrobe transition to the song's drop, accumulating 2.4M likes. The visual withholds the reveal until the beat hits.

Engagement

Comment sections were overwhelmingly reacting to the lyric, not the video. Grammar debates, linguistics takes, "what does this even mean" threads — all of which extended the content's life cycle far beyond the initial post window.

Distribution

Spotify changed its autoplay algorithm during this period to recommendations-based, and "Espresso" benefitted. The Chicken Shop Date green screen meme (31M views) launched a second viral wave months after release, resetting the distribution clock.

Audio Portability

The sound traveled into contexts completely detached from Sabrina Carpenter: bachelorette videos, cooking content, workplace humor. The lyric became a cultural placeholder — people used the sound to signal a vibe, not a fandom.

Core Insight

The most portable audio isn't the most musical moment — it's the most quotable one. "Espresso" went viral on a grammatical glitch, not a melody.

02
Chappell Roan
"Good Luck, Babe!" — 2024
Platform-Resistant Hit

The Setup

"Good Luck, Babe!" is the most analytically interesting case of 2024 because it succeeded despite the algorithm, not because of it. UMG's ongoing TikTok licensing dispute meant there was no official sound clip on the platform at launch. The song still went viral. It debuted at #77 on the Hot 100, climbed to #4, and crossed 1 billion Spotify streams by November 2024.

#4 Billboard Hot 100 Peak
1B+ Spotify Streams
#77→#4 Chart Climb Over Months
6× Plat RIAA Certification

Framework Breakdown

Hook

The Coachella performance was the hook — not a TikTok post. The live performance clip spread organically because the spectacle (maximalist production, Roan's stage presence, the medieval costuming at the VMAs) gave people something to post about that didn't require the official audio.

Retention

The song's emotional architecture is a slow burn — a 1980s power ballad structure where the payoff is withheld until the final chorus. That delayed resolution mirrors what high-retention TikTok content does by design. It built tension that live footage captured naturally.

Engagement

Comment sections split between the song and Roan's identity — the queer narrative, the backstory of being dropped by Atlantic, the decade of near-misses. The song carried emotional weight that made commenting feel personal. That personal stakes dynamic is extremely rare.

Distribution

Without an official TikTok sound, the song was distributed through fan-made clips, performance footage, and reaction videos. This created a longer distribution tail — instead of one algorithm spike, there were dozens of independent ignition points over months.

Audio Portability

The song's queer identity narrative made it a vehicle for community signaling. When people used the audio (official or otherwise), they weren't just sharing a song — they were aligning with a perspective. That ideological portability is a distribution multiplier most songs never access.

Core Insight

Platform gatekeeping only kills songs that depend on the algorithm. Songs with enough community gravity create their own distribution infrastructure — dozens of ignition points instead of one.

03
Charli XCX
brat — Album Cycle 2024
Aesthetic-Driven Spread

The Setup

brat is the most unusual case because the unit of virality wasn't a song — it was a color. The lime-green album aesthetic became a TikTok identity system before most people had heard a single track. The album expanded beyond music into a participatory cultural moment: "brat summer," the font, the green, the self-aware messiness.

brat Dictionary Word of the Year 2024
80M+ Views on SNL Parody Alone
∞ Sound Recontextualization Events

Framework Breakdown

Hook

The album art was the hook. A specific shade of green and a lowercase blurry font — instantly recognizable, infinitely reproducible. Before TikTok users could post the audio, they were posting the aesthetic. The visual came first.

Retention

brat content had unusually high completion rates because it invited participation. The "Apple" dance trend, the "brat behavior" debates, the Harris campaign adoption — each new application of the aesthetic reset the retention cycle for a new audience segment.

Engagement

The comment engine was identity-driven: "am I brat?" became the dominant question. That self-categorization impulse generates an extraordinary volume of engagement because it's personal — it's not about the music, it's about the viewer's relationship to the aesthetic.

Distribution

The brat cycle had no central algorithm spike. It had sustained distributed growth across fashion, politics, meme culture, celebrity coverage, and then back to music. Each context introduced a new audience segment with zero decay in the original core fanbase.

Audio Portability

The audio traveled inside the aesthetic — people who had never heard a Charli XCX song used brat sounds because the green background communicated the whole identity system. The sound became a costume. That's the highest form of audio portability: where using the sound is a statement of identity.

Core Insight

When audio is embedded in a visual identity system, it travels as an aesthetic — not a song. The distribution ceiling is no longer "people who like this music." It's "people who want to perform this identity."

03 — Pattern Synthesis

What the Three Cases
Prove Together

Individually, each case study tells a story. Together, they reveal a system. These are the four cross-case patterns that hold regardless of artist, genre, or platform conditions.

01

Confusion outperforms clarity for retention

All three songs contained an element that made audiences stop and process — a grammatical glitch, a political controversy, a color that didn't make sense for pop. Unresolved cognitive friction keeps people in the comments longer.

02

Audio portability is a separate variable from audio quality

The songs that spread furthest weren't the most technically impressive. They were the most usable — they gave people a vessel to perform an identity, signal an affiliation, or land a joke. Portability is a design problem, not a talent problem.

03

Multiple ignition points beat one algorithm spike

The longest-running viral cycles (brat, Good Luck Babe) had no single origin moment. They burned slowly across multiple contexts and communities. The TikTok algorithm optimizes for spikes; the most durable virality routes around it.

04

Comments signal which layer the song is activating

Espresso: comments about the lyric (hook). Good Luck Babe: comments about the artist's identity (engagement). brat: comments about the listener's identity (audio portability). The comment section is the fastest diagnostic for where a song sits in the framework.

04 — Strategic Implications

What Brands &
Labels Should Do Now

These aren't recommendations for going viral. Virality isn't a strategy — it's an outcome. These are structural shifts in how music content should be built, released, and measured given what the data actually shows.

01 Design for loops, not endings — the payoff that satisfies kills rewatches Production
02 Engineer one portable moment per release — a lyric, a visual, a motion — that travels independent of the song Release Strategy
03 Measure audio uses and duet/stitch behavior, not just plays — those metrics predict the distribution tail Analytics
04 Build an identity layer into the release: the audience needs something to perform, not just something to listen to Brand Strategy
05 Treat platform friction as a distribution variable — Good Luck Babe proved that being blocked from TikTok can be survived if community gravity is strong enough Platform Risk
06 Watch the comment section before spending on ads — it tells you which layer is working and what to amplify Optimization
