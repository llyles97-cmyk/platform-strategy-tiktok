**Mechanism: Platform-Resistant Hit**

## Framework Analysis

### Layer 01 — Hook
- The Coachella performance was the hook — not a TikTok post
- Live performance clip spread organically because the spectacle gave people something to post about that didn't require official audio
- Medieval costuming at the VMAs generated a second visual hook wave
- No official TikTok sound clip available at launch due to UMG/TikTok licensing dispute — the song hooked audiences through live performance footage instead

### Layer 02 — Retention
- Song's emotional architecture: 1980s power ballad structure where the payoff is withheld until the final chorus
- Delayed resolution mirrors what high-retention TikTok content does by design
- Live footage captured this naturally — audiences waiting for the chorus climax in real time
- Emotional buildup → delayed release → rewatch to experience the release again

### Layer 03 — Engagement
- Comment sections split between song and artist identity:
  - The queer narrative
  - The backstory of being dropped by Atlantic Records
  - A decade of near-misses before breakthrough
- The song carried emotional weight that made commenting feel personal
- Personal stakes dynamic is extremely rare — most songs don't make commenting feel like self-disclosure

### Layer 04 — Distribution
- Without an official TikTok sound: distributed through fan-made clips, performance footage, reaction videos
- This created a longer distribution tail vs. a single algorithm spike
- Dozens of independent ignition points over 6 months vs. one platform-dependent launch moment
- Chart trajectory reflects this: #77 → #4 over months, not weeks

### Layer 05 — Audio Portability
- Song's queer identity narrative made it a vehicle for community signaling
- Using the audio = aligning with a perspective, not just enjoying a song
- Ideological portability: a distribution multiplier that most songs never access
- Sound traveled through LGBTQ+ community networks independent of mainstream TikTok

---

## Key Insight

> Platform gatekeeping only kills songs that depend on the algorithm. Songs with enough community gravity create their own distribution infrastructure — dozens of ignition points instead of one.

---

## Contextual Notes

- UMG/TikTok dispute background: UMG pulled its catalog from TikTok in early 2024 over royalty disputes, meaning many major label releases had no official sound clips during the critical launch window
- Roan's label (Amusement Records, distributed through Island/UMG) was affected
- This case became an accidental natural experiment: what happens to a strong song when the primary distribution channel is removed?
- Answer: community gravity matters more than platform access when the song has genuine resonance
